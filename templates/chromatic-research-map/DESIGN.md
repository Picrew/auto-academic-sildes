# Chromatic Research Map

Use when projects, papers, benchmarks, or public artifacts should feel like a
research landscape rather than a resume.

- Use a pale green field, dark ink, and a single coral signal for active claims.
- Organize content as paths, regions, labels, and specimens.
- Let matrices and proof-atlas slides explain relationships, not just list
  topics.
- Color should encode categories or source state. Random color chips are noise.
- Keep evidence large enough to inspect; source plates are the visual terrain.

Anti-patterns:

- fake maps or decorative geography when the content has no actual structure
- tiny screenshots scattered as mood texture
- more than one accent family competing with the proof
